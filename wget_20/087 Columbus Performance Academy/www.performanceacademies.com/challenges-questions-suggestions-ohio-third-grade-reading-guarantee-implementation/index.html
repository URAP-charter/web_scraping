Challenges, Questions, and Suggestions for Effective Implementation of the Ohio Third Grade Reading Guarantee

04-25-2014 | Myrrha Satow

Ohio educators, charter and traditional alike, are in the throes of overseeing high stakes testing on the state mandated Ohio Achievement Assessments (OAA) this week. Â Meanwhile, many are already looking ahead to next year and considering the requirements, implications, and proper implementation of the new Third Grade Reading Guarantee.

The new Third Grade Reading Guarantee incorporates many researched based instructional practices of which Ohio educators can be proud.Â  The spirit of this sweeping legislation is good and right for kids.Â  Every child should be able to read by the time he or she completes third grade.Â  There is ample literature and evidence to support that a child who cannot read by the end of third grade is at risk for poor achievement in other subjects, as well as dropping out of school later on (See Annie Casey Foundation study, 2013). The parts of this new mandate that emphasize early identification, early intervention and literacy represent important shifts in the way Ohio educators think about improving reading achievement in grades K-3. Creating a safety net early and delivering quality reading interventions to the students who need it the most are critical. Too many children in Ohio can’t read by the time they finish third grade. That is unacceptable.

However, practical challenges loom for educators in implementing portions of the Third Grade Reading Guarantee as it currently stands. Practical Implementation Challenges for Schools

1) A significant number of kindergarten students who have not attended preschool or Headstart may qualify for a reading intervention plan (known as a RIMP) once assessed on the new Kindergarten Readiness test in the fall.Â  The early identification of language and literacy deficits in kindergarteners on this new kindergarten test represents a move in the right direction (see, Deeper Assessment for Ohioâs KindergartenersâColumbus Dispatch, April, 2014)

However, the need for intensive interventions for large numbers of kindergarteners under the new provisions of 3GRG may be great for some districts and charters. This means more qualified reading teachers, more qualified interventionists and support staff must be hired quickly to delivery the required interventions in the fall.Â  Schools will have to amp up their rosters over the summer to be prepared. This may be particularly financially burdensome for small traditional school districts or charter schools with small budgets or small numbers of staff where there is a high need for intervention.

2) Summer enrollment efforts and building class rosters over the summer may present significant challenges for schools who accept transfer students from other districts. There is no âvirtual recordâ for students in Ohio, so when a student transfers to another district or to another charter, the new school may be unaware if the child passed the OAA in third grade or not until the sending district submits those scores in response to a records request from the new school.Â  Therefore, a child could potentially be placed in a 4th grade classroom, when in fact under the new 3GRG, he/she should have been retained in the 3rd grade with intensive reading interventions.Â  Test results for the OAA reading test that 3rd graders are taking now will not be available until mid to late June.Â  Grade cards alone will not have information about student passage or failure on the reading OAA.

3) Charters in particular may face difficulties related to proper placement of students in the appropriate grade in the summertime when students transfer in from traditional districts.Â  Many charters receive heavy enrollment over the summer. For some, summer enrollees represent about 1/3 or more of their enrollment, particularly if they are in growth mode.Â  Often, traditional districts do not respond to records requests from charters in July, and charters may not receive student records until fall or well after school starts. Â This is particularly true for charters with early start dates. It is not unusual for a charter school office manager or principal to physically go to a district office to request records in person following multiple requests for records in the summer.Â  This is not a slam against traditional districts.Â  It is simply plainly observed experience for many Ohio charters in many districts. (This also presents significant challenges for proper implementation of IEPs for students in special education, but that is a piece for another day.)

4) The challenges related to receiving timely records and test results may also impact a schoolâs ability to solidify a staff roster and to allocate appropriate classroom space.Â  This will be particularly tricky for charters and small traditional districts to sort out before the first day of school. For example, if a charter school is full in 3rd grade and erroneously enrolls a student in 4th grade due to lack of a virtual record for a student who actually failed the 3rd grade reading OAA, it may prove challenging to pivot and move a student down to a 3rd grade class that is full after school starts.

5) Building a calendar with a reasonable and effective number of instructional days for 2014-2015 may be challenging for traditional public and charter public schools alike.Â  For example, our academic calendar this year reflected 28 allotted testing days for students.Â  Next yearâs calendar has 78 days of testing allotted to meet the new requirements of 3GRG and the new PARCC assessments associated with the Common Core.Â  While every child won’t necessarily test every single day, significant amounts of testing will be occurring each of those days in all of our buildings. Significantly more human and capital resources will be spent on testing. Enough said.

6) Building a calendar with a reasonable number of PLC days may be challenging for some schools for the same reason as above.Â  PLC days are âprofessional learning community daysâ. 8) The big kahuna:Â  Not all students with disabilities will be exempt from the retention requirement of the 3GRG.Â  Presently, Â it appears only students with severe cognitive delays or severe disabilities will be exempt from the retention component of the 3GRG.Â  In practice, IEP teams are being actively discouraged from exempting students with other disabilities from the retention requirement. Practical, Moral, and Research-Based Instructional Implications for Potentially Retaining Â Large Numbers of Ohio Third Graders Next Year

There is no evidence that retention as an intervention improves reading achievement over time. Let me say that again.Â  There is no evidence that retention improves reading achievement over time. As someone trained 20 years ago in the field of school psychology, it was hammered into my brain that nearly a century of research strongly indicates student retention as an intervention for reading achievement was literally 0 for retention to hundreds against.Â  Meta analyses of student retention studies (empirical studies of hundreds of studies over decades on one variable–retention) tell us retention doesnât improve reading achievement.Â  In fact, the unintended consequences of retention are real and troubling, including the very thing we seek to avoidâhigher drop out rates, not to mention poor social adjustment, poorer attendance, and more behavior problems (see also, Annie Casey study, 2013, above). We therefore must carefully consider the long term impact of potentially retaining hundreds, perhaps thousands of third graders next year.Â  They may indeed become the high school dropouts of the future.

Today, more than 20 years later after I was in graduate school studying school psychology, special education, and school policy, it is still the position of the National Association for School Psychologists (NASP) that educators should use methods other than grade retention (and social promotion) to ensure that all students succeed (see NASP retention policy)
http://www.nasponline.org/about_nasp/positionpapers/GradeRetentionandSocialPromotion.pdfÂ .

Suggested alternatives to retention and social promotion from NASP include:

-Intensive, evidence based instruction and intervention to meet the needs of all students

-Equitable opportunities to learn for students from diverse backgrounds

-Universal screening for academic, behavioral, and social-emotional difficulties

-Frequent progress monitoring and evaluation of interventions

These are good things.Â  These components are reflected in the current 3GRG language and should be kept and implemented faithfully.Â  We should be proud in our state to be emphasizing these components.

The whole premise from NASP is that âhaving students repeat the same grade with the same low quality instruction will yield no improvement for the studentâ.Â  So, why would we punish kids for prior poor instruction by retaining them?Â  The consequence (retention) is in the wrong place,Â as the 3GRG currently stands. Â I am proud of the track record over more than a decade in our schools of âun-retainingâ students who came to us from districts having been abhorrently retained not once, but in many cases twice, and in a few cases, three times, while getting them the special education and intensive intervention services they actually needed.

Therefore, we should keep the strong language in the 3GRG in Ohio requiring early identification and early reading intervention for students who need it.Â  We should remove the requirement for retention altogether.Â  There is no basis in research to support retention as an effective intervention.Â  At the very least, we should exempt the retention requirement for all students with disabilities or consider retention earlier (ie kindergarten) in extreme circumstances to ensure we arenât creating far worse problems down the road. The focus moving forward should be on the early identification and delivery of high quality instruction to all students.

Indeed, every child should be able to read by third grade.Â  However, long term, we need to think about a consequence for all schools for failure to teach kids to read. It should be a consequence that does not punish students for their failure to read by third grade and does not risk their future by retaining them.
