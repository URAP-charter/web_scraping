Directions: You have been given five (5) options for your end of unit project for unit 2. Please select one (1) and follow the directions for it. Each project has a picture as a referenced example and a rubric attached to it, so you know what we are looking for in order to receive the highest grade. Before turning in the project please be sure to grade yourself using the rubric, after your teacher grades and returns it, you can compare the two grades. No project option should be completed on loose-leaf (if you need computer paper ask for it), except the diary entries if you choose to handwrite them. You can use online resources to help you complete any of these projects (i.e. story board and others like it.) If you have further questions email us: jfrench@newworldprep.org or lgrissler@newworldprep.org.

 

Project Choice 1:

Salva’s diary:

We learned Salva’s story through Linda Sue Park’s writing. What more might we learn if we heard the story from Salva’s point of view?

For this option:

  • Choose 3 events from Salva’s story.
  • Write at least one paragraph (10 sentences each) about each event
  • Write from the first person point of view
  • Stay true to the character and his experiences

 

Project Choice 2:

Nya’s diary:

Even though Nya is a fictional character, she is based on the people that Water for South Sudan, the non profit organization founded by Salva Dut, builds wells for. What was her experience like in A Long Walk to Water?

For this option:

  • Choose 3 events from Nya’s story.
  • Write at least one paragraph (10 sentences each) about each event
  • Write from the first person point of view
  • Stay true to the character and her experiences

Project Choice 3:

Plot Timeline:

Salva’s story covers a long span of time and takes place in different places which ends in it overlapping with Nya’s story.

For this option:

  • Draw a timeline of Salva and Nya’s stories that show the events in the plot
  • Label where the different events take place
  • For three of the events include an image that depicts what is happening

 

Project Choice 4:

Character Profile:

We don’t know exactly what Salva or Nya looked like in the novel, but we know a few details – their ages and culture – that can help us picture them.

For this option:

  • Draw a picture of either Salva or Nya.
  • Use details in the text to help you imagine what the character looks like
  • You can also draw an object that represents the character (like a large bucket of water to represent Nya’s long journey.)
  • Include details from the text that helped you picture and therefore, draw the character the way you did

Project Choice 5:

Science Project:

When Nya’s younger sister, Akeer, becomes sick with an unnamed waterborne disease, her family travels several days to a clinic, where, after just two doses of medicine, she is “nearly her old self again.” Using books and online resources, you will need to research a waterborne illness and create an illustrated brochure that shows and discusses the bacterium or organism in detail. Some examples of water borne diseases include malaria and cholera.

For this option:

  • Research a waterborne disease
  • Show and discuss:
  • name of bacterium or organism and a picture of it (page 1)
  • what the bacterium or organism looks like and how it infects people (page 2)
  • the disease’s symptoms (page 3)
  • how it is treated (page 4)
  • what happens if it is not treated (page 5)
  • how the illness can be prevented (page 6)
  • Include at least 2 – 3 images (printer from computer or hand drawn)

When I turned eight years old, I began to tend some small calves on my own. I cleaned them, nursed them when they were sick and led them to the very best pastures and watering holes. Soon one became many.

Too many to count.

Before war came, I had never seen so many people in one place. My village had only one hundred people. Now I was in a moving village with thousands of boys.

Like me, the other boys were away from their villages tending their cattle when war came. The adults and girls had stayed behind.

Some of the boys were only five years old. The oldest boys were not more than fifteen. We were children, not used to caring for ourselves. Without out parents we were lost. We had to learn to take care of one another.

The older boys decided to have a meeting.

‘We must work together if we are to survive,’ one of the boys said. ‘We will form groups and choose a leader for each group.’

‘Garang Den!’ someone yelled. My name!

I had been chosen to lead a group of thirty-five boys. I was proud but scared. I knew how to take care of animals, not boys, but I did not want to let my fear keep me from helping my brothers. Then I remembered what my father had told me: Garang, be brave. Your heart and mind are strong. There is nothing you cannot do.

One year later, it happened again. This time the helicopter was smaller and very close overhead. The people inside were looking out of it. I didn’t know what they were looking for.

Soon after the helicopter, raiders came to our village. There was no warning. Sometimes the elders heard that the northerners were coming and we would leave the village and take out cows to another place. But this attack came suddenly and horribly. Explosions, horses and camels chasing people, shooting, screaming, crying: it was like the end of the world.

I deserted my goats and ran into the bush to hide with other people. Smoke billowed out form our village and hung it like a cloud. The roofs of the huts shot up in flames like torches. People scattered to escape the bullets. I watched as the invaders tied the arms and legs of their captives and put long ropes around their necks. They led them form the village on a line blindfolded so they didn’t know the place they were going. ‘Drowning them in the river’ a person cried. ‘They don’t want to waste bullets.’

That night I saw my lovely village Juol, full of palms and coconut trees, fade in a way I couldn’t understand.

The survivors told us not to go back to Juol because there was nobody alive except the enemy. We moved in the darkness, skirting the long grass and not minding the snakes. The fear inside us was acute. All I could think of was my family. I wanted to go home, but the adults wouldn’t let the children return. ‘It’s not safe,’ they said. ‘You’ll be captured.’

With no food and little water, we trekked a hundred miles, staying away from Toch, where the Sudanese government had its army. After four days walking we reached Tonj County. People were gathering there because it was under rebel protection. This is when I recognized my half brother Peter sitting on the road crying. Peter’s mother was my father’s fourth wife and they lived with his grandmother in another village. I knew his village had probably been attacked too” (109).
