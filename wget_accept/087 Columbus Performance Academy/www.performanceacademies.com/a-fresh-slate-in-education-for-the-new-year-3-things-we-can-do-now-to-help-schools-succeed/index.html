A fresh slate in education for the New Year–3 things we can do now to help schools succeed

01-15-2014 | Myrrha Satow

Every New Year brings with it the hope of new ideas in education, new ways of knowing, a fresh slate of clean, clear ideas for teaching. Every New Year for the past 20 years, I have entered it hoping, almost childlike, for a fresh wave of positive thinking and new ideas in education, chock full of new ways education leaders might collaborate for the betterment of children through school choice of every kind. Yet, today all one has to do is open a newspaper (or check Flipboard or any other array of e-News, blogs, or Twitter feed, like I do every morning) and see a fresh spate of “eduslamming” across both sides of the aisle. More often than not, the eduslamming is fraught with misinformation, regardless of where you sit on the issues. Only this morning there is yet another prickly article about charter schools in the Columbus Dispatch. For once, they get it right, rightfully calling for a small number of Sponsors (Authorizers) of charter schools to stop signing up unprepared, inexperienced, or more rarely, but nonetheless sometimes true, scurrilous folks who have no business running schools. (We are particularly proud and fortunate to have excellent sponsors across two states who provide strong support, guidance, and hold our schools accountable in significant and meaningful ways.)

Signing up inexperienced people or unprepared groups with insufficient resources, however well intentioned, is negligent on the part of a small handful of sponsors. This has a dreadful and punitive effect on many other well intentioned, experienced, and thoughtful professional leaders in education, including sponsors, charters, and traditional public schools alike. I argue that all bad press in education is ultimately bad for all educators. It, of course, makes the job extremely difficult for those doing a good job running healthy, high quality schools when schools get bad press, and it makes it devastatingly more difficult when in reaction, legislators potentially bear down on all schools with even more with new regulations, new hammers, and more bureaucracy in response. Good traditional districts and good charters alike cannot operate effectively in a heavily regulated, heavily politicized environment. They simply cannot. The job of properly educating students is too enormous, too complex, and too important to be constantly dealing with political side shows and distractions. While it would be a simple fix to simply stop “letting the bad charters in”, on a policy level (and a practical level) it’s clearly more difficult to address existing failing traditional districts that cannot ever be shut down. Though many courageous school leaders and policy makers are indeed successfully transforming previously failing districts in cities such as Detroit, New Orleans, Washington, DC, Cleveland, Cincinnati, and elsewhere.

There are many real casualties when charter schools start and quickly fail. Yet, there are arguably significantly more casualties when huge, behemoth traditional districts consistently churn out students who either don’t graduate high school, or if they do, come out with few or no skills for college. School failure is most assuredly and most devastatingly harmful to the children who attend both types of failed schools and for the local communities where the schools reside. It is arguably harmful to those seeking to start a charter school who may risk their entire life savings for a well meaning venture yet are permitted to do so with poor guidance, no experience, little support, few resources, and a dearth of legal and financial roadmaps to show them how to make the new school succeed.

I suspect many do not realize that to even start a charter school, they will need significant start up capital and resources to keep the school running in the first three years. Unlike traditional public school leaders, and more like small business entrepreneurs, at any point along the way, new charter operators will likely be required to spend their own resources, mortgage their own home, take out a bridge loan(s), and/or personally guarantee facilities construction. They may be asked to personally finance new construction or renovations for a school building, or, at times when state payments come late, they might be forced to take out a bridge loan to, for example, make the first payroll or subsequent payrolls, pay leases on behalf of the school, and more. That isn’t written anywhere in the fine print when one dreams of developing a charter school. One might be successful at putting his or her vision forward and procuring a charter to open, but one must be careful what one asks for. Schools are not necessarily guaranteed revenues that will come on time, especially in the first year of operations. Developers must have access to capital and strong reserves to carry them through those times, and all too often, the same set of skills required to launch a school aren’t necessarily the same set of skills necessary to operate it and vice versa.

There are no local tax levies for charters, no earmarked facilities funds, no large cash reserves to rely on when the wheels of government might move slowly or dollars do not flow to schools in the same manner as districts. It is true many grants and private funds are organized well in advanced by “elite” charter groups who sometimes have backers with deep pockets. But, for the garden variety charter operator, accessing those funds is often not possible. Yet charters must comply with expensive mandates and the same rules pertaining to special education, service delivery, and many education laws as traditional districts. (Yes, charters must comply, no “ifs, ands, or buts”, despite decades of propaganda to the contrary.) Successfully starting a charter school is indeed “hard” and not for the faint of heart.

More importantly, when a new school quickly crashes and burns a year or two into its launch, this has a negative impact on broader education as a whole, not just on charters, but on all of education. Failure in education of any kind fans the negative public flames and keeps the “eduslamming” going. Similarly, when Columbus Public (or any traditional public school district) is embroiled in testing and financial scandals, it helps no one. Though I do find myself wondering if traditional districts were held to the same standard as charter schools, how many of them would close as well?

Somehow, some way, we need to find a way to help all schools succeed. Period. We surely can’t do it by expending precious time and resources on “eduslamming”, sorting, ranking, blasting, shaming, and hating. Here are three things we can do now to take the first steps of the New Year to rally schools for success:

1. Stop “eduslamming” now. Make a commitment to stop fighting. Say it out loud, say it today, say one positive thing about a different kind of school in your community, be it a charter or a traditional public school. Reach out to another principal or teacher in your area and share ways you’ve been successful! Begin to collaborate outside your comfort zone!
2. Remind a teacher or colleague he/she is a professional. Say something today to a teacher to remind him or her they are truly doing the most important job on the planet!
3. Spread the good news about your school. If a newspaper won’t print your good news, share it on social media, blogs, Facebook, Twitter, wherever you can tell your story! Share it today!

When I got into the world of school choice nearly 20 years ago, I truly believed in exactly that—-school choice. What a powerful dream it was to think that every child might be able to access a great school no matter where they lived, no matter what their learning style! It wasn’t about destroying public education as we know it, and it wasn’t about sorting, ranking, shaming, and tearing down teachers of any stripe–charter school teachers or traditional public school teachers. It was about providing more choices for kids and families. When districts fail in their own way (not by closure, but by academic or financial mismanagement), and when charters fail, we aren’t creating great choices for kids. Rather, we are ultimately continuing the arguments. Education continues to be fodder to sell newspapers. How does that help kids? It’s time to engage students, and it’s time to engage one another in positive, thoughtful discussions about what works for our students. Let’s turn the conversation around by creating and sharing the successes of our kids in 2014!
