What’s the Single, Most Important Thing We Should Do To Ensure Future Success of School Reform?

11-19-2013 | Myrrha Satow

What is the one most important thing we can do right now as school leaders and teachers to ensure the success of our reform efforts?

Tell our story. If I could wave a magic wand, every person involved in school choice, charter schools, and school reform would be telling their success story. In Ohio alone, we are over 400 charter school leader voices and thousands more teacher voices. At over 130,000 charter school students served in this state, there are over a quarter million parents, grandparents, board members, and staff voices yet to be heard. So many are doing all the right things to improve instruction and service delivery in schools–using data, personalizing instruction, and implementing many instructional strategies that will effectively teach our students the 21st century skills they need to be successful in the marketplace.

But we’ve got to let the world know the GREAT things that are happening in our schools. Share your awesome data in simple, understandable terms that show your students’ learning gains. Share your individual success stories. Share your triumphs. Don’t let hatemongering newspapers, Diane Ravitches, teacher unions and schizophrenic think tanks (who change their policies like fashion designers toss out last season’s new dress) define you. You’re your own story.

Use social media. Get on Twitter, Facebook, and Linked In. That’s where parents are. That’s where teachers are. That’s where the future is. Tell your story there. If the newpapers won’t print your good news, tell your story some other way. For far too long now we’ve allowed things to be “done to” and “about” charter schools and school choice. Whether it’s attempted death by a thousand cuts from policy makers, or purposeful edupolicy shaping by newspapers and think tanks, we can’t ignore it.

We need to lead the improvements ourselves and stop allowing other organizations to denigrate our efforts, be they foundations or policy wonks who’ve NEVER taught a single day in their lives, certainly not led a school, nor led a school improvement team for academically at-risk youth. They most certainly have not experienced leveraging their assets, taking unbelievable risks to finance buildings, or exercised the entrepreneurial creativity necessary to successfully open or run a charter school. But theyre going to define us? Certainly not.

So again, if we are to ensure the place for charters into the future, we need to tell our own story. There are many, many people who stand at the ready to kill charter schools and school choice. They revel in negativity. The newspapers still largely publish only bad news because it sells. We need 10 positive stories out there for every bad one. Many well organized teacher unions are again calling for removal of charter schools only this week. They are organized and complaining to legislators that we “take too many resources from traditional districts”. We need a common voice. I am sure we have more commonalities within the school choice movement than differences. We need leadership, and we need a plan. The fact is, “making charter schools go away” isn’t going to solve the public school district’s problems, because their problems so often have no consequences. They don’t close, and they can go on in perpetuity and miseducate or undereducate thousands of children. Ours in the ultimate in educational accountability—the possibility of school closure. That’s what we signed up for. That’s what we embrace. But let us tell our story. Let us tell our truths.

One old persisting canard is that we waste taxpayer money. But, when we perform the same or better, than the traditional districts, we save the taxpayers smoney. We don’t take their resources, we are saving money. Charters leverage no local property tax dollars and rely almost exclusively on state and federal funding and funds they manage to raise themselves. Certainly the day that charters receive funding equitably with districts will be a great day for charter school students.

Let’s define ourselves. If for no reason other than our teachers, our staff, your children, they need us to lead. They are in the trenches every day; they are being tested every day. They need our leadership so they can do their job. Let’s define ourselves. Continue to create the data driven teams that drill down to the individual student level, and continue creating a safety net of academic and behavioral supports for those students who need it the most.

Parents are coming to us for higher academic quality, but they come for many other reasons as well-—safety, smaller class sizes, better communication with their child’s school, more individualized attention, a specific niche like the arts, fitness, or technology. Together, we have to help promote and defend a parent’s right to choose the best education for their child. One size most certainly does not fit all. Share your triumphs. Tell your story. Protect a parent’s right to choose a quality school that meets their child’s needs.
